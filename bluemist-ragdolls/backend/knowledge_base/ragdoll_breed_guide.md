# Ragdoll Breed Guide — BlueMist Ragdolls Knowledge Base

## Overview
The Ragdoll is a large, semi-longhaired cat breed known for its docile temperament and tendency to go limp when picked up — hence the name "Ragdoll." They are one of the largest domestic cat breeds and are beloved for their affectionate, dog-like personalities.

## History
The Ragdoll breed was developed in the 1960s by Ann Baker in Riverside, California. The breed was created by selectively breeding domestic longhaired cats and is now recognized by all major cat registries including TICA (The International Cat Association) and CFA (Cat Fanciers' Association).

## Temperament
Ragdolls are often described as "dog-like" cats. Key traits include:
- Extremely docile and gentle — they rarely scratch or bite
- Very affectionate and people-oriented
- Follow their owners around the house like dogs
- Go limp when held (the classic "ragdoll" behavior)
- Get along exceptionally well with children and other pets
- Low aggression — not well-suited to outdoor life because they are too trusting
- Quiet — they have soft, melodic voices and are not demanding
- Slow to mature — they reach full size at around 4 years of age

## Size
Ragdolls are one of the largest domestic cat breeds:
- Males: 15–20 lbs, sometimes larger
- Females: 10–15 lbs
- Full growth takes approximately 4 years

## Colors and Patterns

### Base Colors
- Seal (dark brown/black points)
- Blue (gray points)
- Chocolate (milk chocolate points)
- Lilac (pinkish-gray points)
- Red / Flame (orange points)
- Cream (pale orange points)

### Patterns
- **Colorpoint**: Darker color on face, ears, legs, and tail. White/cream body.
- **Mitted**: Like colorpoint but with white mittens on front paws, white boots on back legs, and a white chin/chest stripe.
- **Bicolor**: White inverted V on face, white legs, white belly. Points on ears, tail, and outer face.
- **Lynx**: Tabby striping overlaid on any of the above patterns.

## Coat and Grooming
- Semi-longhaired, silky, and plush
- Minimal undercoat — less prone to matting than many longhaired breeds
- Weekly brushing recommended to prevent tangles
- Shed moderately — more in spring and fall
- Do NOT shave a ragdoll — their coat helps regulate temperature

## Health

### Common Genetic Conditions
**HCM (Hypertrophic Cardiomyopathy)**: The most important health concern in ragdolls. HCM causes thickening of the heart muscle and is the leading cause of cardiac disease in cats. Responsible breeders DNA test for the HCM gene mutation (MyBPC3). Cats should be tested "HCM negative" or "clear."

**PKD (Polycystic Kidney Disease)**: A genetic condition causing cysts in the kidneys. Also testable via DNA. All reputable breeders should test for PKD.

### General Health Notes
- Ragdolls are generally a healthy, robust breed when bred responsibly
- Annual vet checkups recommended
- Keep indoor only — their trusting nature makes them vulnerable outdoors
- Vaccinate and keep up with parasite prevention
- Watch for weight — they can become overweight
- Lifespan: 12–17 years with proper care

## Nutrition
- High-quality protein-first diet recommended
- Wet food is beneficial for kidney health
- Avoid grain-heavy fillers
- Fresh water always available — many ragdolls prefer running water (consider a fountain)
- Monitor portions — they can overeat

## Kitten Development
- Eyes open: 7–14 days
- Begin eating solid food: 3–4 weeks
- Litter box training: 3–4 weeks (they catch on quickly)
- Socialization window: 2–7 weeks (critical period)
- Go to new homes: minimum 12 weeks
- Full color development: 2 years
- Full size: 4 years

## Living with a Ragdoll
- They thrive on companionship — consider getting two if you work long hours
- Intelligent but not destructive
- Enjoy interactive play — wand toys, puzzle feeders
- Love to be near their people but are not typically "lap sitters" for the first year
- Indoor only — non-negotiable for responsible ownership
- Get along with dogs, other cats, and respectful children
